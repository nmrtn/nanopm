## 1. Problem summary
Even with a list of user problems in hand, a solo builder has no repeatable way to decide which one is worth building against next. Prioritization is gut-feel and not tied to strategic impact, so what gets built can't be shown to be the highest-leverage problem — and the reasoning isn't durable enough to revisit or defend later.

## 2. Value to the user
### Job to be done
When the builder has a set of opportunities, they want to rank them by their best impact for the company's strategy — so the next thing they build is demonstrably the most valuable problem to solve, not just the one that feels urgent. The alternative today is an in-the-moment gut call that leaves no trace and drifts from the strategy.
### Where we fall short
**Prioritization is gut-feel, not strategy-tied**
There's no consistent basis linking each problem's rank to the company's actual objectives, so the ordering reflects mood and recency more than leverage.

**The ranking isn't durable or revisitable**
A gut call leaves no record, so the builder can't re-examine why a problem was deprioritized or update the order as strategy shifts.

**Can't demonstrate the choice was highest-leverage**
Without an explicit, strategy-anchored ranking, the builder can't show — to themselves or anyone else — that they're building the right problem rather than a convenient one.

## 3. Value to the company
Phase 3B (prioritize by strategic impact) is what makes the loop closed rather than just productive: it ties the chosen problem back to strategy so the build phase isn't working blind. A persisted, strategy-linked ranking reinforces the schema-validated-state retention bet and is the input the build handoff depends on.
